# [The World](#the-world)

The Uncharted Waters aren't endless. Sail far enough in any direction and you'll hit **the Great Fog**, a wall of mist no ship has ever sailed through and returned to tell about. At the center of the sea sits something worse: **the Deep One**, a whirlpool that never stops churning, wide enough to swallow a fleet. Whether it's a force of nature or something aware down there is a question nobody has answered. Keep it that way until your table needs an answer.

## What Happened

Generations ago, there was **The Cataclysm**...

Nobody alive remembers the cause. What they agree on is the result: the sea became trapped inside the Fog in a whirling current around the Deep One, and for generations, nothing left and nothing came back. Ships are only now daring back out past the harbors that survived. That makes this a world being **remapped**, not discovered for the first time. Old charts exist. Some are still accurate. Some describe islands that sank, forests that grew over ruins, or ports that don't answer hails anymore. Let your players find out which is which the hard way.

One open thread worth keeping in your back pocket: an advanced civilization once left magical technology scattered across these waters, the kind your Deep Crawlers and Horizon Chasers are still digging up. Whether that civilization caused the cataclysm, was destroyed by it, or has nothing to do with it at all is unresolved. Any of the three makes for a good campaign-length mystery.

## How Magic Works Here

Most folks in the Uncharted Waters are born with a specific magical affinity.

- A **Storm**-touched navigator can feel a lightning strike coming before the sky even darkens, and the Horizon Chasers won't sail without one aboard.
- An **Air**-touched deckhand can coax a dying breeze back into slack sails, and "wind-blessed" crew command a hiring bonus at every port in the sea.
- **Illusion**-touched grifters are the reason the Siren's Rest's gambling dens check a winning hand twice before paying out.

Technology here has kept pace with magic rather than being replaced by it. Ships are wind-driven by air magic instead of oar or sail alone, tools and weapons run up to an early gunpowder era, and airships exist but are rare and expensive enough that seeing one is an event. None of this needs numbers at your table either. It's flavor for how NPCs react to the party, not a system to track.

## Who's Out There

The sea is full of people who want things from your players, because your crew answers to nobody. Merchants and pirates are locked in a feud that's dragged every ship into picking a side. Druids and industrialists are one bad season from open war over who owns the ocean floor. Scavengers and scholars fight over who gets to dig up the past. Explorers are racing to fill in the map.

Every one of those groups gets its own chapter in this booklet. Whichever faction your players stumble into first, someone there already has a job for them.
